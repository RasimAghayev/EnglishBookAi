# 🔶 PART 8 · Chapter 40
# 200 Native Transformations — textbook → natural
### *the same meaning, said the way natives actually say it — 200 upgrades in 10 moves* 🔄

> **The one-line idea.** Your English can be **100% correct and still sound "textbook."** Fluency is the last mile: swapping stiff, literal, or wordy phrasing for the **natural** version. This chapter gives **200 transformations** in **10 moves** — read the left column (correct but flat), learn the right (how a native says it).
> 🇦🇿 *İngiliscən **100% düzgün ola bilər, amma yenə "dərslik" kimi səslənə bilər.** Səlislik son mərhələdir: quru, hərfi və ya artıq ifadəni **təbii** variantla dəyişmək. Bu fəsil **10 hərəkətdə 200 çevrilmə** verir — sol sütun (düzgün, amma yeknəsəq), sağ sütun (doğma).*

## 🎯 In this chapter you will learn 10 native "moves"…
```
① stiff/formal → natural spoken          ⑥ plain → idiomatic
② Latinate verb → phrasal verb            ⑦ basic adjective → vivid/precise
③ direct → polite / indirect              ⑧ neutral → emphatic (cleft/do/inversion)
④ wordy → concise                          ⑨ textbook connector → native discourse marker
⑤ learner-literal → native collocation    ⑩ full form → reduction/contraction (spoken)
```
> 🇦🇿 *Bu fəsildə 10 doğma "hərəkət": rəsmi→təbii, Latın fel→phrasal, birbaşa→nəzakətli, artıq→yığcam, hərfi→kollokasiya, sadə→idiomatik, adi sifət→canlı, neytral→vurğulu, dərslik bağlayıcı→doğma marker, tam→qısaldılmış.*

## 🅱️ §B — NATIVE-SPEAKER INSIGHT

1. **Shorter usually wins.** Natives prefer *"because"* to *"due to the fact that,"* *"soon"* to *"in the near future."* Concise = confident. 🇦🇿 *Qısa adətən qalib gəlir.*
2. **Phrasal verbs are the native default.** *"Put off, look into, find out"* sound natural where Latinate verbs sound written. 🇦🇿 *Phrasal fellər doğma standartdır.*
3. **Idioms are seasoning.** A few make you sound fluent; too many sound like you swallowed a phrasebook. Deploy sparingly. 🇦🇿 *İdiomlar ədviyyatdır — azı fluent göstərir, çoxu süni.*
4. **"very + adjective" is a missed upgrade.** *"Very tired" → "exhausted"* instantly lifts your English. 🇦🇿 *"very + sifət" əvəzinə güclü sifət.*
5. **Reductions are for the ear, not the pen.** *Gonna/wanna* are normal speech but wrong in writing — and *"should of"* is always wrong. 🇦🇿 *Qısaltmalar qulaq üçün, qələm üçün yox.*
6. **Discourse markers create flow.** *"So…, Anyway…, That said…, Mind you…"* are the glue of natural conversation. 🇦🇿 *Diskurs markerləri təbii axını yaradır.*

## 🅳 §D — DEEP-ANALYSIS TASKS

```
1. TRANSFORM (Latinate → phrasal): postpone · investigate · tolerate · establish · discover.

2. POLITE-IFY: "Give me the report." · "Repeat that." · "You're wrong." · "Wait."

3. TRIM (wordy → concise): "due to the fact that" · "at this point in time" · "a large number of."

4. UPGRADE the adjective: "very tired" · "very hot" · "very happy" · "very boring."

5. EMPHASISE (cleft/do/inversion): "Sara called." · "I need time." · "I've never seen it."

6. 🇦🇿→NATIVE EN: "Şəkil çəkdim." (not "make a photo") · "Səhv etdim." (make/do?) ·
     "İşığı yandır." (open/turn on?) — give the native collocation.
```
> ✍️ For each, keep the meaning, change the style. Say your answers aloud — natural English has a rhythm.

# 📖 MINI STORY #1 — "The Textbook Speech → The TED Talk"

### *One paragraph, twice: first "correct but stiff," then transformed into natural, native English.*

---

**📖 Version A — correct but textbook:**

> *Good evening. I am of the opinion that we must make a decision on this matter at this point in time. Due to the fact that our costs have increased, it is necessary for us to reduce expenditure. Moreover, we must investigate new markets. In order to succeed, it is of great importance that everyone makes an effort. In conclusion, I am confident that we will achieve success in the near future.*

**🗣️ Version B — transformed to natural:**

> *Good evening. **If you ask me**, we need to **decide** on this **right now**. **Because** our costs have **gone up**, we've **got to cut back** on spending. **What's more**, we need to **look into** new markets. **To succeed**, it **really matters** that everyone **pulls their weight**. **So, all in all**, I'm confident we'll **make it** **soon**.*

```
        ├─ too long?      → TRIM (move ④)
        ├─ too Latinate?  → PHRASAL (move ②)
        ├─ too blunt?     → SOFTEN (move ③)
        ├─ too literal?   → COLLOCATION (move ⑤)
        └─ too flat?      → VIVID / IDIOM / EMPHASIS (moves ⑥⑦⑧)
```

> 🔑 You already speak *correct* English — this filter is the **last 10%** that turns it *native*. Run it on your own writing. 🇦🇿 *Bu filtr düzgün İngiliscəni doğma edən son 10%-dir.*

## 🅴2 — PHRASAL VERBS deep (separable vs inseparable)

| Type | Rule | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **separable** | object can go in the middle | turn **the light** off / turn off **the light** |
| separable + pronoun | pronoun **must** go in the middle | turn **it** off *(not ~~turn off it~~)* |
| **inseparable** | object stays after | look **after the baby** *(not ~~look the baby after~~)* |
| 3-part | fixed order | put up **with** it / look forward **to** it |

> ⚠️ With a **pronoun**, separable phrasals split: *"put it off," "turn it on," "throw them away"* — never ~~put off it~~. 🇦🇿 *Əvəzlik ilə ayrılan phrasal fellər bölünür: "put it off".*

## 🅴3 — COLLOCATION clusters (make · do · take · have · get)

| Verb | Common collocations |
|---|---|
| **make** | a mistake, a decision, a plan, progress, money, an effort, a mess, friends, a promise |
| **do** | homework, the housework, a job, business, research, someone a favour, your best, the shopping |
| **take** | a photo, a shower, a break, a seat, a risk, a chance, notes, care, place, your time |
| **have** | breakfast, a shower, a rest, a good time, a look, a chat, fun, a party |
| **get** | ready, dressed, married, lost, better, in touch, a job, home |

> 🔑 These verbs are **not interchangeable**: *make a decision* (not ~~take~~ in most varieties — though *take a decision* exists in formal BrE), *do homework* (not ~~make~~), *take a photo* (not ~~make/do~~). Learn as fixed chunks. 🇦🇿 *Bu fellər bir-birini əvəz etmir — birləşmə kimi öyrən.*

|---|---|

## 🅴5 — INTENSIFIER upgrades (very → strong adjective → absolutely)

```
   "very + ordinary adjective"  →  ONE strong adjective  →  "absolutely/utterly + strong adjective"
   very tired    →  exhausted    →  absolutely exhausted
   very good     →  brilliant    →  absolutely brilliant
   very bad      →  awful        →  absolutely awful
```

| Weak | Strong (non-gradable) | Booster |
|---|---|---|
| very cold | freezing | absolutely freezing |
| very surprised | astonished | utterly astonished |
| very funny | hilarious | absolutely hilarious |
| very tired | exhausted | completely exhausted |

> ⚠️ Use *absolutely/utterly* (not *very*) with **strong/non-gradable** adjectives: *"absolutely freezing"* ✅, ~~"very freezing"~~ ✗. 🇦🇿 *Güclü sifətlərlə "very" yox, "absolutely/utterly": "absolutely freezing".*

## 🅴8 — HEDGING & softening (natural politeness)

| Blunt | Softened (native) |
|---|---|
| It's wrong. | It's not quite right. |
| I disagree. | I'm not sure I agree, actually. |
| That's expensive. | It's a bit on the pricey side. |
| You're late. | You're running a little late. |
| No. | I'd rather not, if that's ok. |
| I don't like it. | It's not really for me. |

> 🔑 Natives cushion opinions with *a bit, sort of, kind of, not quite, I'm afraid, actually* — directness can sound rude even when correct. 🇦🇿 *Doğma danışanlar fikri "a bit, kind of, I'm afraid" ilə yumşaldır.*

## 🅴10 — REGISTER matching (when NOT to go casual)

| Context | Go natural-casual? |
|---|---|

## 🅴12 — 🇦🇿 CONTRASTIVE SYNTHESIS (why AZ learners sound "textbook")

| Cause | Effect | Cure |
|---|---|---|
| **calquing** (word-for-word) | "make a photo," "open the light" | learn native collocations |
| avoiding **phrasal verbs** | over-formal "investigate/postpone" | swap in "look into/put off" |
| **directness** (AZ is more direct) | "Give me…," "You're wrong." | add modals + hedges |
| **verbosity** from formal AZ | "due to the fact that…" | trim to "because" |
| few **idioms** learned | flat, literal English | add 1–2 idioms, sparingly |

> 🧠 **The Azerbaijani contrast in one line:** learners sound "textbook" not because their grammar is wrong but because they **translate structure and words directly** from Azerbaijani — producing correct-but-unnatural English (*calques* like "make a photo"), avoiding **phrasal verbs** and **idioms** that have no neat Azerbaijani equivalent, and carrying over Azerbaijani's greater **directness**. The cure is to stop translating and start **collecting native chunks**: whole collocations (*take a photo*), phrasal verbs (*look into*), softeners (*a bit, I'm afraid*), and a handful of idioms. This chapter's 200 pairs are exactly that collection.

# 📖 MINI STORY #2 — "The Phrasebook Tourist"

### *A visitor speaks stiff, textbook English; a friendly local shows him how people really talk.*
### 🇦🇿 *Qonaq quru, dərslik İngiliscəsi ilə danışır; mehriban yerli ona insanların həqiqətən necə danışdığını göstərir.*

> Read it twice. **First** for the story; **second** noting each textbook line and its natural upgrade.
> 🇦🇿 *İki dəfə oxu: əvvəl hekayə üçün, sonra hər dərslik sətri və onun təbii variantını qeyd edərək.*

---

Daniel had learned English from a very old textbook. On his first day in London, he walked into a café.

"Good morning. I would like to **purchase** a coffee," he announced. *(stiff)*

The barista smiled. "Sure — you can just say *'Could I get a coffee?'* Milk?"

"That is affirmative," said Daniel. *(stiff)*

"We just say *'Yes, please.'*" She laughed kindly. "You'll pick it up."

At the next table, a local named Zara struck up a chat. "First time here?"

"Indeed. I am **desirous of** visiting the museum, but I am **not able to** locate it," Daniel said. *(stiff)*

"Ah — you'd say *'I'd love to see the museum, but I can't find it.'* It's easy — take the second left. **It's a piece of cake**." *(idiom)*

"I **comprehend**," said Daniel.

"*'Got it'* is what we'd say," Zara grinned. "And instead of *'It is raining heavily,'* we just say *'It's pouring.'* Instead of *'I am extremely fatigued,'* say *'I'm shattered.'*"

Daniel repeated them, delighted. "So, if I understand… *'I'm gonna grab a coffee, then I'm off to the museum'*?"

"**Now** you sound like a local!" Zara said. "Your grammar was always perfect — you just needed the **natural** version."

By the end of the day, Daniel had stopped *purchasing* things and started *grabbing* them, stopped being *fatigued* and started being *shattered*, and — best of all — stopped sounding like a textbook.
