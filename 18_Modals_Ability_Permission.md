# 🟥 PART 4 · Chapter 18
# Modals (1) — Ability & Permission
### *can · could · be able to · may · might* — "I **can** swim." / "**May** I come in?" 🔓

> **🟥 PART 4 başlayır — Modal fellər (Modals & Semi-Modals).** İngilis dilində *can, could, may,
> might, must, should, will, would, shall* kimi kiçik amma çox güclü sözlər var. Bunlar **bacarıq,
> icazə, ehtimal, məcburiyyət, məsləhət** və s. bildirir. Onların öz **xüsusi qrammatikası** var
> (heç vaxt *-s* almır, sonra **bare infinitive** = V1 gəlir, *do/does/did* işlətmir). Bu fəsil
> modalların **qızıl qaydaları** ilə başlayır, sonra ilk iki funksiyanı öyrədir: **bacarıq**
> (*can/could/be able to*) və **icazə** (*can/could/may/might*). 🔓

> **Azərbaycanca qısa giriş:** İki əsas ideya: **(1) BACARIQ** — *"bacarıram / edə bilirəm"*
> (*I can swim* = üzə bilirəm). Keçmişdə: *could* (ümumi bacarıq) və *was able to* (konkret bir dəfə
> bacardım). **(2) İCAZƏ** — *"olar / icazə var"* (*Can I…? Could I…? May I…?*). *may/might* daha
> rəsmidir. Gəl başlayaq. 😊

## 0. 🔑 The Golden Rules of ALL Modals / Bütün modalların qızıl qaydaları

Before the meanings, learn the **shared grammar** — it's true for *every* modal in Part 4:

```
 1. NO -s in the 3rd person:   ✅ She can swim.    ❌ She cans swim.
 2. Followed by the BARE infinitive (V1 — no "to"):  ✅ I can go.  ❌ I can to go.
 3. NO do/does/did in questions/negatives — invert the modal itself:
       ✅ Can you swim?   ❌ Do you can swim?
       ✅ He can't swim.   ❌ He doesn't can swim.
 4. NO two modals together:   ❌ I will can…  →  ✅ I will be able to…
 5. Same form for all persons:  I/you/he/she/we/they + can (no change).
 6. For tenses a modal lacks, use a SEMI-MODAL substitute:
       can → be able to     ·     must → have to     ·     (no "to + can")
```
> 🇦🇿 Bu 6 qayda Part 4-ün **bütün** fəsillərinə aiddir. Modallar "tənbəl" fellərdir: dəyişmir,
> sonra sadə fel (V1) gəlir, *do/does/did* sevmir, iki modal yan-yana gəlmir.

## 1. 🧭 Introduction / Giriş

A **modal verb** adds *meaning* (ability, permission, possibility…) to a main verb. This chapter
covers the first two jobs:

```
   ABILITY ("can do it")        →  can · could · be able to
   PERMISSION ("allowed to")    →  can · could · may · might
```
🇦🇿 **Modal fel** əsas felə əlavə məna verir. Burada iki iş: **bacarıq** və **icazə**.

---

## 2. ❓ Why do we use them? / Niyə işlədirik?

| Function | Modal | Example | 🇦🇿 |
|----------|-------|---------|-----|
| Present ability | **can** | I **can** drive. | Sürə bilirəm. |
| Past general ability | **could** | I **could** run fast as a kid. | Uşaqlıqda sürətli qaça bilirdim. |
| Past *single* achievement | **was/were able to** | We **were able to** escape. | Qaça bildik (bir dəfə). |
| Future ability | **will be able to** | You **'ll be able to** join soon. | Tezliklə qoşula biləcəksən. |
| Permission (informal) | **can** | **Can** I sit here? | Bura otura bilərəm? |
| Permission (polite) | **could** | **Could** I leave early? | Tez gedə bilərəmmi? |
| Permission (formal) | **may** | **May** I come in? | İçəri girə bilərəmmi? |
| Permission (very formal) | **might** | **Might** I make a suggestion? | Bir təklif edə bilərəmmi? |

## 3. 🌍 Real-life situations / Həyati vəziyyətlər
```
   • Talking about skills:      "I can cook, but I can't bake."
   • Childhood abilities:        "She could read at the age of four."
   • A one-off success:          "After hours, they were able to fix it."
   • Asking to do something:     "Can I borrow your pen?" / "May I be excused?"
   • Giving/refusing permission: "You can go." / "I'm afraid you can't park here."
   • Polite requests:            "Could you pass the salt?" (request, not permission)
```

## 4. ⏳ Timeline — ability across time / Bacarıq zaman boyu
```
   PAST (general)     PAST (one time)        PRESENT          FUTURE
   could / couldn't   was/were able to       can / can't      will be able to
   "I could swim."    "I was able to swim    "I can swim."     "I'll be able
                       across the lake."                        to swim soon."
   ─────●──────────────────●───────────────────●─────────────────●──────────►
```
> ⚠️ **Big rule:** for a **positive single past achievement**, use **was/were able to**, NOT *could*.
> But **couldn't** works for both general and single past inability. 🇦🇿 Müsbət, tək keçmiş uğur →
> *was able to*; ümumi/tək uğursuzluq → *couldn't*.

## 5. 🏗️ Formula / Formul
```
 ABILITY
   can/could + V1            I can swim.   I could swim.
   be able to + V1           I am/was/will be/have been able to swim.
   (−) can't / couldn't / be not able to     I can't swim.  I wasn't able to swim.
   (?) Can / Could + S + V1?  Can you swim?  Could you swim then?

 PERMISSION
   can/could/may/might + V1   Can/Could/May/Might I + V1 ?
   (giving)  You can/may + V1        You can go.   You may leave.
   (refusing) can't / may not / mustn't    You can't / may not / mustn't smoke here.
```

## 7. 📐 Grammar Rules / Qaydalar

1. **can = present** ability/permission; **could = past** ability OR **polite present** permission/request.
2. **Single past success → *was/were able to*** (= *managed to*), not *could*: ✅ *I was able to
   pass the exam.* ❌ *I could pass the exam* (for one specific success).
3. ***could* IS fine** for: (a) **general** past ability (*I could swim as a child*), (b) past ability
   with **senses/thinking verbs** (*I could see/hear/smell/understand…*), (c) **negatives**
   (*couldn't* = both general & single).
4. **be able to** fills the gaps *can* can't reach: future (*will be able to*), perfect (*have been
   able to*), infinitive (*to be able to*), after another modal (*might be able to*).
5. **Permission politeness ladder:** *can* (informal) < *could* (polite) < *may* (formal) < *might*
   (very formal/tentative).
6. **Refusing permission / prohibition:** *can't* (general) · *may not* (formal) · *mustn't* (strong
   prohibition — it's forbidden).
7. ***Could you…?* / *Can you…?*** = a **request** (asking someone to act), distinct from permission
   (*Can I…?*).

## 8. ⚔️ The ability system — can / could / was able to / Bacarıq sistemi
```
   PRESENT ability ......... can           "I can drive."
   GENERAL past ability .... could          "I could drive at 17."
   ONE-TIME past success ... was/were able to "I was able to start the old car."  (NOT "could")
   ONE-TIME / general FAIL . couldn't / wasn't able to "I couldn't start it."
   FUTURE ability .......... will be able to "I'll be able to drive after my test."
   PERFECT/INF/after-modal . be able to       "I've been able to / to be able to / might be able to"
```
> 🔑 Memory hook: **"could = used-to-be-able; was able to = managed (once)."**
> 🇦🇿 *could* = ümumiyyətlə bacarırdım; *was able to* = (bir dəfə) bacardım/uğur qazandım.

## 10. ⚠️ Common Mistakes / Ümumi səhvlər

| ❌ Mistake | ✅ Correction | Why / 🇦🇿 |
|-----------|--------------|-----------|
| She **cans** swim. | She **can** swim. | modal heç vaxt *-s* almır |
| I can **to** drive. | I can drive. | modaldan sonra V1 (no "to") |
| **Do** you **can** help? | **Can** you help? | modalda *do* işlənmir |
| I will **can** come. | I **will be able to** come. | iki modal olmaz → *be able to* |
| Yesterday I **could** fix it (one success). | …I **was able to** fix it. | tək keçmiş uğur → *was able to* |
| **May** you pass the salt? | **Can/Could** you pass the salt? | xahiş → *can/could*, *may* yox |
| You **mustn't to** park here. | You **mustn't** park here. | *mustn't* + V1 |

## 11. 🔀 Comparison — the permission ladder / İcazə pilləkəni
```
   LESS FORMAL ───────────────────────────────────► MORE FORMAL
   Can I…?         Could I…?        May I…?          Might I…?
   (friends)       (polite)         (formal/respectful) (very formal, rare)
   "Can I go?"     "Could I go?"    "May I go?"       "Might I go?"

   GIVING:    "You can…"  (neutral) · "You may…" (formal)
   REFUSING:  "You can't…" (general) · "You may not…" (formal) · "You mustn't…" (prohibition)
```
> 🇦🇿 İcazə istəyəndə yuxarı qalxdıqca daha nəzakətli/rəsmi olur. *Can I* dostlar arasında, *May I*
> rəsmi yerlərdə (məs. müsahibə, müştəri).

### Ability vs Permission — same word *can*!
```
   "I can swim."        → ABILITY (I have the skill)
   "You can swim here." → PERMISSION (it's allowed)
   Context decides which meaning. 🇦🇿 Kontekst mənanı müəyyən edir.
```

## 13. 🔊 Pronunciation notes / Tələffüz qeydləri
```
   can  → usually WEAK /kən/ in statements:  "I can swim" → /aɪ kən swɪm/
   can't→ STRONG /kɑːnt/ (BrE) /kænt/ (AmE), with a clear /t/:  "I can't swim."
   ⚠️ The difference between "can" and "can't" is mostly the VOWEL + stress, not the /t/!
      Positive "can" is unstressed; "can't" is stressed and longer.
   could → /kʊd/ ; couldn't → /ˈkʊdnt/ ;  "could've" /ˈkʊdəv/ (not "could of")
   may /meɪ/ ; might /maɪt/
```
> 🌟 Listening tip: if the verb is **stressed and short-voweled**, it's probably **can't**.
> 🇦🇿 *can* zəif (/kən/), *can't* güclü və uzun — fərqi saitdə və vurğuda axtar.

## 22–26. 🎤 Skills tips
```
 SPEAKING:  Default to "Can I…?" with friends, "Could I…?" / "May I…?" with strangers & staff.
 WRITING:   Formal writing prefers "may" for permission and "be able to" for ability/achievements.
 LISTENING: Train your ear for weak "can" /kən/ vs strong "can't" — the vowel/stress, not the /t/.
 READING:   Signs & rules use "may not / must not" for prohibition ("Visitors may not enter").
```

## 28. ❓ FAQ
**Q: *could* or *was able to* for the past?** A: General ability → *could*; one specific success →
*was able to*. Negative → *couldn't* works for both.
**Q: Is *can I…* wrong for permission?** A: No — it's standard and natural; *may I* is just more formal.
**Q: *Can you…?* vs *Can I…?*** A: *Can you…?* asks someone to do something (request); *Can I…?* asks
for permission for yourself.
**Q: Two modals?** A: Never. Use a semi-modal: *will be able to*, *might be able to*.

```
 ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
```

# 📖 Mini Story — *"The Locked Door"* (≈250 words)

Aysel **could** pick almost any lock. As a teenager she **could** open the garden gate, the shed,
even her father's old desk — a harmless party trick. But she had never met a door like this one.

"**Can** I try?" she asked the museum guard, half joking. The vault behind the glass had jammed, and
the curator was frantic. "Nobody **can** open it. The locksmith **can't** come until tomorrow."

"**May** I see the mechanism?" Aysel said, suddenly serious.

The guard hesitated. "I'm not sure visitors **are allowed to**—"

"**Could** she just *look*?" the curator cut in. "We **can't** wait until morning; the alarm goes off
at six and we **won't be able to** reset it without the key inside."

They let her kneel by the lock. For twenty minutes she **couldn't** feel anything. Then — a tiny
click. "I think I **can** do it," she whispered.

She **wasn't able to** turn it at first. Her hands shook. *Breathe,* she told herself. With one last,
careful twist, she **was able to** ease the bolt back.

The door swung open.

The curator gasped. "How **can** I thank you?"

Aysel smiled and stood. "You **can** start," she said, "by telling me what's inside."

He laughed. "I'm afraid I **can't** — that, you **may not** see. Some doors stay shut for a reason."

# ✂️ Chunking · 🎧 Shadowing · 🧠 Memory Tricks
```
 CHUNK:   [Can/Could/May + I + V1?]   ·   [was able to + V1]   ·   [will be able to + V1]
 SHADOW:  copy polite service English: "Could I take your name?" "May I help you?"
 TRICK:   🪜 PERMISSION LADDER: Can → Could → May → Might (informal → formal)
          🎯 "could = used-to-be-able · was able to = managed (once)"
          🚫 "No -s, no 'to', no 'do', no double modal."
```

# 🗣️ B. NATIVE-SPEAKER INSIGHT
```
 • WEAK "CAN":     in speech "can" is reduced to /kən/; the stressed, longer one is usually "can't".
 • "CAN I GET…":   very common (esp. AmE) for ordering: "Can I get a coffee?" (= I'd like).
 • SOFTENERS:      "Could you possibly…?", "Do you think you could…?" — extra-polite requests.
 • "MAY" feels formal: everyday speech prefers "Can I…?"; "May I…?" signals politeness/service.
 • "MIGHT I…?":    rare, very formal/old-fashioned — used for effect ("Might I suggest…").
 • "CAN'T" for refusal: "Sorry, you can't" is normal; "you may not" sounds official/strict.
 • ABILITY perfect: natives say "I haven't been able to…", never "I haven't could…".
 • "COULD'VE":     spoken "could have" = "could've" — never write "could of".
```

# ♻️ C. ALTERNATIVE EXPRESSIONS

Expressing **ability** ("I can do it") and **permission** ("Can I…?") in other ways:

|-------------|----------|-----|

| I**'m able to** swim. | slightly formal | ability (also fills tense gaps) |

| **Can/Could/May I** sit here? | ladder | asking permission |

### Grammar Detective — find & fix, then explain *why*

### Compare the meanings


### Choose the most natural version
```
 e) Ordering coffee: (i) "May I get a latte?"  (ii) "Can I get a latte?"
```

## ✅ Deep-Analysis Answer Key
```
 Detective:
 1. "He can PLAY the guitar." (modal + bare infinitive, no "to")
 2. "CAN you come tomorrow?" (no "do" with a modal)
 3. "…I was finally ABLE TO meet the director." (single past success → was able to)
 4. "She will BE ABLE TO join us later." (no double modal)
 5. "CAN/COULD you help me carry this?" (request → can/could, not may)

 Compare:
 a) "could run 10 km" = general past ability;  "was able to run 10 km that day" = one specific
    achievement (managed it on that occasion).
 b) "You can go" = neutral permission;  "You may go" = formal permission (teacher/official register).
 c) "Can I help you?" = I offer to help you;  "Can you help me?" = I ask you for help.
 d) "can't park" = it's not possible/allowed (general);  "mustn't park" = it's prohibited (a firm rule).

 Choose:
 e) (ii) "Can I get a latte?" — natural for ordering (esp. AmE); "May I get" is unusual.
 f) (ii) "Could I possibly have Friday off?" — more polite/appropriate for a request to a manager.
```

# 🧩 EXPANSION PACK — Ability & Permission (deep dive)

> A deeper layer: the full *be able to* paradigm, the perception-verb exception, *could have* (past
> unrealised ability), the request & offer systems, *be allowed/permitted to*, *can* for general
> possibility, reported speech, the many faces of *can't*, and the politeness pragmatics. 🔓

## E1 · The full *be able to* paradigm / *be able to*-nun bütün formaları
*can* exists only in present (*can*) and past (*could*). For everything else, use **be able to:**
```
   PRESENT ........ am/is/are able to     "She's able to cope."          (or just "can")
   PAST ........... was/were able to      "We were able to escape."      (one success)
   FUTURE ......... will be able to       "You'll be able to drive soon." (no "will can"!)
   PRESENT PERFECT  have/has been able to  "I haven't been able to sleep."
   PAST PERFECT ... had been able to       "She had been able to save up."
   INFINITIVE ..... to be able to          "I want to be able to help."
   -ING / GERUND .. being able to          "Being able to drive is useful."
   AFTER A MODAL .. modal + be able to     "I might be able to come." "You should be able to."
```
> 🇦🇿 *can*-ın yalnız iki forması var (*can/could*). Qalan bütün zamanlarda və modaldan sonra
> **be able to** işlədilir. ❌ *will can* · ❌ *to can* · ❌ *can can*.

---

## E2 · *could* vs *was able to* — the perception/cognition exception / İstisna
General rule: **single past success → *was able to*, NOT *could*.** BUT with **verbs of perception
and mental state**, *could* IS fine even for a single occasion:
```
   ✅ "From the hill I could see the whole city." (perception — could OK)
   ✅ "I could hear someone crying."   "I could smell smoke."   "I could feel the heat."
   ✅ "I could understand / remember / tell what he meant." (mental verbs)
   ❌ "I could fix the car yesterday." (action, single success) → ✅ "was able to fix"
```
And **couldn't covers everything** (general + single, action + perception):
```
   "I couldn't see anything." ✅   "I couldn't open the door." ✅
```
> 🇦🇿 *see, hear, feel, smell, understand, remember* kimi fellərlə tək hadisədə də *could* olar.
> Hərəkət felində tək uğur üçün → *was able to*. İnkar (*couldn't*) həmişə işləyir.

---

## E3 · *could have + V3* — past ability NOT used / İstifadə olunmamış keçmiş bacarıq
```
   "I could have helped you (but I didn't)."   → I had the ability/chance — it didn't happen.
   "She could have won, but she gave up."
   "You could have told me!"                   → reproach: you were able to, yet didn't.
```
Contrast with *was able to* (which means it **did** happen):
```
   "I was able to help."        → I helped (success).
   "I could have helped."        → I didn't help (unrealised).
```
> 🇦🇿 *could have + V3* = "edə bilərdim, amma etmədim" (baş tutmamış). *was able to* = "etdim".
> Bu, gileylənmək üçün də işlənir: *"You could have told me!"* (deyə bilərdin axı!).

---

## E4 · The request system / Xahiş sistemi
Asking **someone else** to do something (not permission for yourself):
```
   LESS POLITE ──────────────────────────────────────────► MORE POLITE
   Can you…?    Could you…?    Would you…?    Would you mind + V-ing?    
   "Can you help?" "Could you help?" "Would you help?" "Would you mind helping?"
   EXTRA-POLITE: "Do you think you could…?" · "I was wondering if you could…" ·
                 "Could you possibly…?" · "Would it be possible to…?"
```
> ⚠️ ***Would you mind helping?*** → agreement = **"No (, not at all)"** (because "mind" = object to).
> 🇦🇿 Xahiş üçün *Can/Could/Would you…?*. *Would you mind + V-ing?* — razılıq "No" (etiraz etmirəm) deməkdir.

---

## E5 · Offers with *Can I…? / Shall I…?* / Təklif
*Can I…?* isn't only permission — it also **offers help**:
```
   "Can I help you?" / "Can I get you anything?"   (offering)
   "Shall I open the window?"                       (offering — BrE, polite)
   "Can I carry that for you?"
```
> 🇦🇿 *Can I…?* həm icazə, həm də **kömək təklifi** ola bilər. *Shall I…?* = nəzakətli təklif (BrE).

---

## E6 · Permission across tenses — *be allowed/permitted to* / İcazə zamanlar üzrə
*can/could/may* don't cover every tense of permission. Use **be allowed to / be permitted to:**
```
   PRESENT:  "We can / are allowed to park here."
   PAST (general):  "As kids we were allowed to stay up late." (NOT "could" for a granted permission rule)
   PAST (one occasion granted):  "Yesterday I was allowed to leave early." (NOT "could")
   FUTURE:   "You'll be allowed to vote at 18."
   PERFECT:  "We've never been allowed in there."
   FORMAL:   "Visitors are permitted to … / are not permitted to …"
```
> 🔑 Like ability, **single-occasion granted permission in the past → *was allowed to*, not *could*.**
> ✅ *I was allowed to go.*  ❌ *I could go.* (the second sounds like ability/hypothetical).
> 🇦🇿 Keçmişdə bir dəfə verilən icazə → *was allowed to* (could yox).

---

## E7 · *can* for general possibility / characteristic / Ümumi imkan
*can* also describes **what is generally possible** or a **typical characteristic** — not a guess:
```
   "It can get very cold here in winter."   (general possibility — it sometimes does)
   "Smoking can cause cancer."               (general truth/capability)
   "Children can be cruel."                  (typical characteristic)
   "This software can crash if overloaded."  (capability)
```
> ⚠️ This is different from **deduction** (*"It must be cold"* = I deduce now) — Chapter 19.
> 🇦🇿 Bu *can* "ümumiyyətlə ola bilər / bəzən olur" deməkdir — konkret təxmin yox (o, Ch.19-dadır).

---

## E8 · Reported speech with these modals / Dolayı nitqdə
When you report, present modals backshift (detail in Ch. 25):
```
   can  → could       "I can swim." → He said he could swim.
   may  → might        "You may go." → She said we might go.
   could/might/would → usually UNCHANGED (already past forms)
   "Can I help?" (offer) → He asked if he could help.
```
> 🇦🇿 *can → could*, *may → might* (dolayı nitqdə geri sürüşür). *could, might* artıq keçmiş formadır,
> dəyişmir.

---

## E9 · The many faces of *can't* / *can't*-ın müxtəlif mənaları
One word, several jobs — context decides:
```
   1. INABILITY:     "I can't swim." (no skill)
   2. NO PERMISSION: "You can't park here." (not allowed)
   3. IMPOSSIBILITY/DEDUCTION: "That can't be true!" (logical — bridges to Ch.19)
   4. REFUSING a request: "Sorry, I can't (do that)."
```
> 🇦🇿 *can't* = (1) bacarmamaq, (2) icazə olmaması, (3) məntiqi qeyri-mümkünlük (Ch.19), (4) imtina.

---

## E10 · Ability synonyms with nuance / Bacarıq sinonimləri
| Expression | Nuance | 🇦🇿 |
|------------|--------|-----|
| **can / be able to** | neutral ability | edə bilmək |
| **manage to** | succeed in something *difficult* | (çətinliklə) bacarmaq |
| **succeed in + V-ing** | formal success | nail olmaq |
| **know how to** | a learned skill | bacarmağı bilmək |
| **be capable of + V-ing** | have the capacity (often potential) | qabiliyyəti olmaq |
| **be in a position to** | have the means/authority (formal) | imkanı/səlahiyyəti olmaq |
| **have the ability/capacity to** | formal | bacarığı olmaq |
```
   "I managed to finish." (it was hard)   vs   "I was able to finish." (neutral)
   "We're not in a position to comment." (formal — we can't/won't)
```

## E11 · Politeness pragmatics / Nəzakət incəlikləri
```
   ASKING PERMISSION, soft → strong-formal:
     "Is it OK if I…?"  <  "Do you mind if I…?"  <  "Could I…?"  <  "May I…?"  <  "Might I…?"
   ⚠️ "Do you mind if I sit here?" → YES-answer = "No, go ahead." ("No" grants it!)
   INDIRECT (very polite): "Would it be all right if I…?", "I wonder if I might…"
   GIVING warmly:  "Of course you can.", "Sure, go ahead.", "By all means." (formal)
   REFUSING gently: "I'd rather you didn't.", "I'm afraid that's not possible."
```
> 🇦🇿 *Do you mind if I…?* sualına razılıq **"No"** ilə verilir (etiraz etmirəm). Bu, azərbaycanlılar
> üçün tələdir!

## E12 · AZ contrastive synthesis / Sintez
| 🇦🇿 | ❌ word-for-word | ✅ natural English | Key |
|-----|------------------|--------------------|-----|
| Sabah gələ biləcəyəm. | I will can come tomorrow. | I**'ll be able to** come tomorrow. | no double modal |
| Dünən maşını düzəldə bildim. | Yesterday I could fix the car. | …I **was able to** fix the car. | single success |
| Səni görə bilirdim. | I was able to see you. | I **could** see you. | perception → could OK |
| Kömək edə bilərdim (amma etmədim). | I could help (but didn't). | I **could have** helped. | unrealised past |
| Tez getməyə icazə verildi. | I could leave early. | I **was allowed to** leave early. | granted permission |
| Bura otura bilərəm? (dostla) | May I sit here? | **Can** I sit here? | informal → can |

```
   ONE-LINE SYNTHESIS:
   skill now → can · general past → could · one success → was able to · future → will be able to
   unrealised past → could have V3 · granted permission (past) → was allowed to
   request → Can/Could/Would you · offer → Can/Shall I · perception past → could (exception)
```

## 11) Minimal pairs — same word, different meaning / cüt nümunələr
```
 a) "I can swim."        (ABILITY — I have the skill)
    "You can swim here."  (PERMISSION — it's allowed)

 b) "Can I help you?"     (I OFFER help)
    "Can you help me?"     (I REQUEST help)

 c) "I could swim at five."        (general past ability)
    "I was able to swim across."    (one specific success)
    "I could have swum across."     (ability I didn't use)

 d) "You can't park here."  (not allowed — general)
    "You mustn't park here." (prohibited — firm rule)
    "That can't be right."   (logical impossibility — Ch.19)
```

# 📖 Mini Story #2 — *"The Driving Test"* (≈250 words)

Kamran had practised for months. He **could** reverse, he **could** park, he **could** read the road
— but **could** he do it all when it mattered?

"**May** I adjust the mirror before we start?" he asked.

"Of course you **can**," said the examiner, a calm woman with a clipboard. "**Can** you tell me when
you're ready?"

His hands were sweating. At home he **could** park perfectly; today he wasn't sure he **would be
able to** do anything.

"**Could** I take a second?" he asked.

"Take your time. You **can't** rush a test."

He breathed, pulled out, and — to his surprise — drove smoothly. He **could** feel the car
responding. When she said, "**Could** you take the next left and pull over," he signalled early and
stopped neatly. *I can do this*, he thought.

Then came the hill start. His worst nightmare. As a learner he had **never been able to** manage it
without rolling back. The car shuddered… but this time he **was able to** hold it steady and pull
away cleanly.

At the end, the examiner closed her folder. "**May** I be the first to say — you've passed."

Kamran couldn't speak.

"From tomorrow," she smiled, "you**'ll be able to** drive on your own. But you **mustn't** forget the
basics — and you **can't** use your phone, ever."

"**Can** I call my mum first?" he laughed.

"That," she said, "you most certainly **may**."
