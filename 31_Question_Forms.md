# 🟪 PART 5 · Chapter 31
# Question Forms — how English asks
### *yes/no · wh- · subject vs object · negative · tag · indirect* — "**Where do** you live?" / "You're coming, **aren't you?**" ❓

> **The one-line idea.** English doesn't ask a question just with the voice — it **re-orders the sentence.** It puts an **auxiliary (the "operator") before the subject**, and if there isn't one, it borrows **do / does / did.**
> *"You live here."* → *"**Do** you live here?"* · *"She is ready."* → *"**Is** she ready?"* · *"He left."* → *"**Did** he leave?"*
> 🇦🇿 *İngiliscə sualı təkcə səslə vermir — **cümləni yenidən düzür.** **Köməkçi feli (operatoru) mübtədadan əvvələ** qoyur; köməkçi yoxdursa, **do/does/did** götürür.*
> 🇦🇿 *Azərbaycanca isə sual sadəcə **-mı/-mi/-mu/-mü** şəkilçisi (və ya sual sözü) ilə düzəlir — söz sırası dəyişmir: "Gəlirsən" → "Gəlirsən**mi**?"*

## 🧭 THE BIG PICTURE — the "operator" and inversion

Every English question turns on one small word: the **operator** — the first auxiliary verb. To ask, you **flip the operator in front of the subject:**

```
   STATEMENT:  Subject  +  [operator]  +  …          "She   can    swim."
   QUESTION:   [operator]  +  Subject   +  …    ?     "Can    she   swim?"

   No operator in the statement? English INSERTS one — do / does / did:
   STATEMENT:  "You   live   here."         (hidden operator)
   QUESTION:   "DO    you    live   here?"  (do appears; main verb goes back to base form)
```

> 🧠 **The key mental move:** find the **operator** (a form of *be*, or an auxiliary like *have/will/can/would…*) and put it **before the subject**. If the statement has **only a main verb** in the present or past simple, bring in **do/does/did** — and the main verb returns to its **base form** (*"Did she **go**?"*, never ~~did she went~~).
> 🇦🇿 *Əsas fikir: **operatoru** tap (be, ya da have/will/can…) və mübtədadan əvvələ qoy. Sadə zamanda operator yoxdursa, **do/does/did** gətir — əsas fel **məsdər formasına** qayıdır.*

> ⚠️ **Do-support only for present/past simple.** If there's already an operator (*is, has, will, can…*), **don't** add *do*: ~~Do you can swim?~~ → *Can you swim?* 🇦🇿 *do/does/did yalnız sadə zamanlar üçün; artıq operator varsa, do əlavə etmə.*

## 📋 THE FORMS — at a glance

| Statement type | Operator | Yes/No question | Wh- question |
|---|---|---|---|
| **be** | is/are/was/were | **Are** you ready? | **Where are** you? |
| **present simple** | *(insert do/does)* | **Do** you know? / **Does** he know? | **What do** you want? |
| **past simple** | *(insert did)* | **Did** they win? | **When did** they win? |
| **present perfect** | have/has | **Have** you eaten? | **What have** you done? |
| **future / modal** | will/can/would/should… | **Will** you come? / **Can** you swim? | **Why should** I go? |
| **continuous** | am/is/are/was + -ing | **Are** you working? | **What are** you doing? |

> 🔑 The wh- word goes **first**, then the ordinary yes/no machinery follows: **Wh- + operator + subject + verb?** 🇦🇿 *Wh- sözü ƏN ƏVVƏL, sonra adi bəli/xeyr quruluşu: Wh- + operator + mübtəda + fel?*

## 🔑 THE CORE — SUBJECT questions vs OBJECT questions

The trap that fools nearly everyone. When *who / what / which* is the **subject** of the verb, you use **normal word order and NO *do***:

```
   OBJECT question (who/what = object):   need an operator + do
        "Who  DID  you   call?"        ← YOU is the subject; who is the object
        "What DID  she   say?"

   SUBJECT question (who/what = subject): NO do, NO inversion — normal order
        "Who   called   you?"          ← WHO is the subject (the caller)
        "What  happened?"    "Who wants tea?"    "Which team won?"
```

| ❓ Object question (with *do*) | ❓ Subject question (no *do*) |
|---|---|
| **Who did** you **invite**? | **Who invited** you? |
| **What did** he **break**? | **What broke**? |
| **Which** book **did** you **read**? | **Which** book **won** the prize? |
| **How many** people **did** you **see**? | **How many** people **came**? |

> 🧪 **Test:** does the wh- word **do** the action? → **subject** question → **no *do***. Does someone else do it **to** the wh- word? → **object** question → **use *do***.
> 🇦🇿 *Sual sözü hərəkəti EDİRSƏ → mübtəda sualı → do YOX. Hərəkət ona EDİLİRSƏ → tamamlıq sualı → do LAZIM.*

> ⛔ Classic error: ~~"Who did break the window?"~~ (if *who* is the culprit) → **"Who broke the window?"** 🇦🇿 *"Who broke…?" — mübtəda sualında do işlənmir.*

## ❓ WH- QUESTIONS in depth

| Word | Asks about | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **who / whom** | person | **Who** is that? / **Whom** did you meet? *(formal)* |
| **whose** | possession | **Whose** bag is this? |
| **what** | thing / info | **What** are you reading? |
| **which** | choice from a set | **Which** one do you prefer? |
| **where / when** | place / time | **Where** / **When** did it happen? |
| **why** | reason | **Why** are you late? |
| **how** | manner | **How** did you do it? |
| **how + adj/adv** | degree | **How old / tall / far / often / long / much / many**…? |

> 🔑 **what vs which:** *which* = a choice from a **limited/known set** (*"Which colour — red or blue?"*); *what* = **open-ended** (*"What colour do you like?"*). 🇦🇿 *which = məhdud çoxluqdan seçim; what = açıq sual.*

> 🗣️ **how + adjective** is one unit: *"**How long** is the film?" "**How often** do you train?" "**How much** is it?"* Don't split it: ~~How long time…?~~ → *How long…?* 🇦🇿 *"how + sifət" bir bütövdür: how long, how often, how much.*

## 🚫 NEGATIVE QUESTIONS — surprise, confirmation & offers

Formed by inverting the **negative** operator (contracted): *Don't you…? Isn't it…? Haven't we…?* They rarely ask for plain information — they carry an attitude:

| Use | Example |
|---|---|
| **surprise / disbelief** | **Don't** you **remember** me?! / **Haven't** you **finished** yet? |
| **seeking agreement** | **Isn't** it a lovely day? / **Wasn't** that film great? |
| **polite offer / suggestion** | **Wouldn't** you **like** some tea? / **Why don't** we **eat** out? |
| **mild criticism** | **Can't** you **be** quiet? / **Shouldn't** you **be** working? |

> ⚠️ **The Yes/No answer trap.** English answers the **fact**, not the question's polarity. *"Aren't you coming?"* → *"**Yes**"* = I **am** coming; *"**No**"* = I'm **not**. Never let the negative flip your answer. 🇦🇿 *İngiliscə **fakta** cavab verir: "Aren't you coming?" → "Yes" = gəlirəm; "No" = gəlmirəm. (Azərbaycanca "hə/yox" suala razılığa görə dəyişir — tələ budur!)*

> 🔤 **"aren't I?"** is the standard contraction for *am I not*: *"I'm next, **aren't I**?"* (~~amn't I~~ is dialectal). 🇦🇿 *"am I not" → danışıqda "aren't I".*

---

## 🏷️ TAG QUESTIONS — the little check at the end

A statement + a mini-question tag. **The rule of opposites:** a **positive** statement takes a **negative** tag, and vice-versa. The tag reuses the **operator**:

```
   POSITIVE statement  →  NEGATIVE tag:   "You're coming,   aren't you?"
   NEGATIVE statement  →  POSITIVE tag:   "You don't smoke, do you?"
        └─ same operator (be / do / have / modal), opposite polarity, subject → pronoun
```

| Statement | Tag | Note |
|---|---|---|
| She **can** drive, | **can't** she? | modal repeats |
| They **left** early, | **didn't** they? | past simple → *did* |
| You **haven't** met, | **have** you? | negative → positive |
| **I'm** late, | **aren't I?** | *am* → *aren't I* |
| **Let's** go, | **shall we?** | *let's* → *shall we* |
| **Open** the door, | **will you?** | imperative → *will/won't/can you* |
| **There's** a problem, | **isn't there?** | *there* repeats |
| **Nobody** called, | **did they?** | negative word + *they* |

> 🎵 **Intonation changes the meaning:** a **rising** tag = a genuine question ("You locked the door, didn't you?↗ — I'm not sure"). A **falling** tag = seeking agreement ("Lovely day, isn't it?↘ — I know it is"). 🇦🇿 *Yüksələn ton = əsl sual; enən ton = razılıq gözləmək.*

> 🔑 Negative words (*never, nothing, hardly, no one*) make the statement **negative**, so the tag is **positive**: *"He hardly ever calls, **does he**?"* 🇦🇿 *never/nothing/hardly cümləni inkar edir → quyruq müsbət olur.*

## 📦 INDIRECT / EMBEDDED QUESTIONS — the word order flips back

When a question sits **inside** another sentence (after *Could you tell me…, I wonder…, Do you know…*), it stops looking like a question: **statement word order, no *do*, no inversion.**

```
   DIRECT:    "Where  IS  the station?"          (inverted)
   INDIRECT:  "Could you tell me where the station  IS?"   (statement order — no inversion)

   DIRECT:    "What   DOES  it mean?"
   INDIRECT:  "I don't know what it  MEANS."      (no 'does'; verb takes the tense)

   DIRECT:    "DID she call?"      (yes/no)
   INDIRECT:  "Do you know  IF / WHETHER  she called?"   (add if/whether)
```

| Direct question | Indirect / embedded |
|---|---|
| Where is he? | Do you know **where he is**? |
| What time does it start? | Can you tell me **what time it starts**? |
| Why did they leave? | I wonder **why they left**. |
| Is it open? | I'm not sure **if it's open**. |
| Can you swim? | She asked **whether I could swim**. |

> ⚠️ **Two big traps:** (1) **don't invert** — ~~"Do you know where is it?"~~ → *"…where it is."* (2) **drop *do/does/did*** — ~~"I wonder what does he want"~~ → *"…what he wants."* 🇦🇿 *Dolayı sualda (1) inversiya YOX, (2) do/does/did YOX — düz cümlə sırası. (Bu, Azərbaycan dilinin təbii sırasına daha yaxındır!)*

> ❓ **Question mark?** Only if the **whole sentence** is a question: *"Could you tell me where it is**?**"* (main clause is a question) vs *"I wonder where it is**.**"* (main clause is a statement). 🇦🇿 *Sual işarəsi yalnız əsas cümlə sualdırsa qoyulur.*

## 🔗 PREPOSITIONS in questions & *who* vs *whom*

In everyday English, the **preposition stays at the end**; in formal English it moves to the front with *whom*:

```
   informal:  "Who   are you talking   TO?"        "What are you looking AT?"
   formal:    "TO whom are you talking?"           "At what are you looking?" (rare)
```

| Informal (preposition at end) | Formal |
|---|---|
| **Who** did you go **with**? | **With whom** did you go? |
| **What** are you afraid **of**? | **Of what** are you afraid? |
| **Where** are you **from**? | *(no formal shift)* |

> 🔑 ***whom*** is the **object** form. Modern English mostly uses *who* everywhere; keep *whom* for formal writing or right after a preposition (*"to whom," "for whom"*). 🇦🇿 *whom = tamamlıq forması; müasir dildə çox vaxt "who"; rəsmi dildə və sözönündən sonra "whom".*

## 🧱 OTHER question patterns

| Pattern | Example | Use |
|---|---|---|
| **short / echo questions** | "I passed!" — "**Did you?**" / "**Really?**" | showing interest / surprise |
| **question-word only** | "**Why?**" / "**Who?**" / "**How come?**" | quick follow-up |
| **How come + statement order** | **How come** you **left** early? | = why (informal, no inversion) |
| **What…for? / What…like?** | **What** did you do that **for**? / **What**'s she **like**? | purpose / description |
| **Or-questions** | Tea **or** coffee? / Are you staying **or** going? | offering alternatives |

> 🗣️ *"How come…?"* keeps **statement order** (*"How come you're late?"*, not ~~How come are you late~~) — a useful exception. 🇦🇿 *"How come…?" = niyə (qeyri-rəsmi), amma düz söz sırası ilə.*

## 🅰️ §A — LEVEL DIFFERENCES (A1 → C2)

How the *same skill* — asking — grows with your level:

| Level | What the learner can do | Typical question |
|---|---|---|
| **A1** | *be* questions; basic *do* + wh- | "**Are** you OK?" / "**What**'s your name?" |
| **A2** | past *did*; common wh-; *how + adj* | "**Did** you **go** out?" / "**How old** are you?" |
| **B1** | subject vs object Qs; basic tags | "**Who** **broke** it?" / "You're new, **aren't you?**" |
| **B2** | negative Qs; indirect Qs; if/whether | "**Isn't** it obvious?" / "Do you know **if it's open**?" |
| **C1** | full tag system; *whom*; preposition placement | "**Let's** start, **shall we?**" / "**To whom** should I write?" |
| **C2** | nuance, register, echo/rhetorical Qs | "**How come** you never mentioned it?" / "**Wouldn't** you just know it!" |

> 🇦🇿 *A1: be + sadə do. A2: keçmiş did, how+sifət. B1: mübtəda/tamamlıq sualları, sadə taglar. B2: inkar və dolayı suallar. C1: tam tag sistemi, whom. C2: incəliklər, exo/ritorik suallar.*

## 🅱️ §B — NATIVE-SPEAKER INSIGHT

1. **Tags are a conversation glue.** Natives sprinkle *"…isn't it? …don't you? …right?"* to invite agreement and keep talk flowing. 🇦🇿 *Quyruq sualları söhbəti canlı saxlayır, razılıq dəvət edir.*
2. **Negative questions are often not real questions.** *"Isn't it gorgeous?"* expects you to agree, not to inform. 🇦🇿 *İnkar sualları çox vaxt razılıq gözləyir, məlumat yox.*
3. **"You know what I mean?" / "…right?"** are modern all-purpose tags, replacing the full auxiliary tag in casual speech. 🇦🇿 *"…right?" müasir universal tag-dır.*
4. **Prepositions live at the end.** *"Who are you going with?"* sounds natural; *"With whom are you going?"* sounds like a lawyer. 🇦🇿 *Danışıqda sözönü sonda; "with whom" rəsmi/sərt səslənir.*
5. **"How come?" beats "Why?" for a puzzled tone.** It's softer and more curious — and keeps statement order. 🇦🇿 *"How come?" — təəccüblü, mülayim "niyə".*
6. **Rising vs falling tags matter.** Get the intonation wrong and a friendly *"…isn't it?↘"* can sound like a genuine doubt *"…isn't it?↗"*. 🇦🇿 *Tonu səhv salsan, məna dəyişir.*

# 📖 MINI STORY #1 — "The Job Interview"

### *Direct questions, indirect questions, tags and one tricky negative question — across the desk.*
### 🇦🇿 *Birbaşa suallar, dolayı suallar, quyruq sualları və bir çətin inkar sualı — masanın o tayında.*

---

Aygün straightened her jacket and knocked. "Come in," said a voice.

"So," the manager began, "**why do** you **want** this job?" *(object wh- question.)*

"Because I love the work, and — may I ask — **what does** the role **involve** day to day?" *(indirect politeness + object question.)*

"Good question. Mostly client calls. **Have you worked** in sales before?" *(present-perfect yes/no.)*

"I have. **Could you tell me how big the team is**?" Aygün asked. *(indirect question — statement order, no inversion.)*

"Six people. **Who trained** you at your last company?" *(subject question — no 'do'!)*

"My manager, Elçin. He taught me everything."

The interviewer smiled. "**You're not nervous, are you?**" *(negative tag — seeking reassurance.)*

"A little," Aygün admitted, "but I'm excited too."

"**Don't** you **find** cold-calling difficult?" the manager pressed. *(negative question — testing her.)*

"**No** — I actually enjoy it." *(she answers the fact: 'No, I don't find it difficult.')*

"I like that. **Do you know when you could start**?" *(indirect — 'when you could start,' not 'when could you start.')*

"Whenever you need me."

"Then let me ask one last thing — **you can start on Monday, can't you?**" *(positive statement, negative tag.)*

Aygün grinned. "**Monday? Absolutely.**"

### 🔍 Spot the question — sort them out

| From the story | Type | Note |
|---|---|---|
| "**Why do** you **want** this job?" | object wh- question | *do*-support |
| "**What does** the role **involve**?" | object wh- question | base verb after *does* |
| "**Have you worked** in sales?" | yes/no (present perfect) | invert *have* |
| "Could you tell me **how big the team is**?" | indirect question | statement order |
| "**Who trained** you?" | **subject** question | no *do* |
| "You're not nervous, **are you?**" | tag (neg. statement → pos. tag) | reassurance |
| "**Don't** you **find** it difficult?" | negative question | testing / surprise |
| "**No** — I enjoy it." | answer to negative Q | answers the **fact** |
| "Do you know **when you could start**?" | indirect question | no inversion |
| "You can start Monday, **can't you?**" | tag (pos. → neg.) | modal *can* repeats |

1. Find the two indirect questions. Rewrite each as a direct question (with inversion).
2. "Who trained you?" — why no 'did'? What kind of question is it?
3. The manager asks "Don't you find cold-calling difficult?" and Aygün says "No." What exactly does
   her "No" mean? Rewrite it as a full sentence.
4. Add a suitable tag: "You've done sales before, ______?" and "Let's meet again next week, ______?"
5. 🇦🇿→EN: "Komandanın neçə nəfər olduğunu deyə bilərsinizmi?" / "Səni kim öyrətdi?" / "Bazar ertəsi başlaya bilərsən, elə deyil?"
```
> 💬 **Answers**
> 1. *"Could you tell me how big the team is?"* → **"How big is the team?"** · *"Do you know when you could start?"* → **"When could you start?"** (direct = inversion returns).
> 2. It's a **subject question** — *who* is the person who did the training (the subject), so English uses **normal order and no *do***: *"Who trained you?"* (not *"Who did train you?"*).
> 3. Her "No" answers the **fact**: *"No, I don't find cold-calling difficult."* (English answers the situation, not the negative in the question.)
> 4. *"You've done sales before, **haven't you?**"* · *"Let's meet again next week, **shall we?**"*
> 5. Could you tell me how many people are in the team? / Who trained you? / You can start on Monday, can't you?

# 🧨 EXPANSION PACK — Question Forms (E1–E12)
### *The deep layer: the operator system, subject-vs-object, tags in full, indirect order, reported questions & echoes.*
### 🇦🇿 *Dərin qat: operator sistemi, mübtəda↔tamamlıq, tam tag cədvəli, dolayı sıra, nəql edilən suallar və exo.*

---

## 🅴1 — The OPERATOR system (the engine of every question)

An English clause has a hidden slot for an **operator** — the first auxiliary. Questions, negatives and tags all use it. The trick:

```
   STATEMENT              OPERATOR         QUESTION
   "She is here."      →  is           →  "Is she here?"
   "They have left."   →  have         →  "Have they left?"
   "He will come."     →  will         →  "Will he come?"
   "You like it."      →  (none!) → do →  "DO you like it?"     ← do fills the empty slot
```

- If an auxiliary is **present** (be, have, will, can, should…), just **move it** in front of the subject.
- If the verb is a **bare present/past simple**, English **inserts do/does/did** and the main verb drops to its **base form**.

> 🇦🇿 *Hər cümlədə gizli **operator** yeri var. Operator varsa — mübtədadan əvvələ keçir; sadə zamanda yoxdursa — do/does/did gətirilir və əsas fel məsdərə qayıdır.*

---

## 🅴2 — ⚠️ SUBJECT vs OBJECT questions (the do / no-do splitter)

The exam classic. Compare the two roles of *who*:

```
   "Who   CALLED   Tom?"        →  WHO is the SUBJECT (the caller) → NO do, normal order
   "Who   DID Tom  CALL?"       →  WHO is the OBJECT (the called)  → USE do
```

| Question | Who/what is… | Form |
|---|---|---|
| **Who broke** the window? | the subject (breaker) | no *do* |
| **Who did** you **invite**? | the object (invited) | *do* |
| **What caused** the delay? | the subject (cause) | no *do* |
| **What did** the delay **cause**? | the object (result) | *do* |
| **Which horse won**? | the subject (winner) | no *do* |
| **Which horse did** you **back**? | the object (backed) | *do* |

> 🧪 **Test:** if the wh- word is the **doer**, ask straight (no *do*). If someone acts **on** it, use *do/does/did*. 🇦🇿 *Sual sözü **edən**dirsə — do yox; hərəkət **ona** edilirsə — do var.*

---

## 🅴3 — do / does / did & the "one tense-marker" rule

Only **one** word in the verb phrase carries tense and number. When *do* takes it, the main verb goes **bare**:

| Statement | Question | Why |
|---|---|---|
| She **likes** jazz. | **Does** she **like** jazz? | *does* carries -s → *like* is bare |
| They **went** home. | **Did** they **go** home? | *did* carries past → *go* is bare |
| He **studies** hard. | **Does** he **study** hard? | never ~~does he studies~~ |

> ⛔ Double-marking errors: ~~Does she likes?~~ / ~~Did you went?~~ → *Does she **like**? / Did you **go**?* 🇦🇿 *Zaman/şəxs yalnız BİR sözdə: do/does/did onu daşıyanda əsas fel məsdərdə qalır.*

---

## 🅴4 — NEGATIVE questions in depth (& the answer trap)

| Function | Example | Real meaning |
|---|---|---|
| surprise | **Haven't** you **finished** yet? | I expected you had |
| seek agreement | **Isn't** she **clever**? | I think she is — agree with me |
| polite offer | **Won't** you **sit down**? | please do |
| suggestion | **Why don't** we **go**? | let's go |
| reproach | **Can't** you **be** quiet? | please stop |

> ⚠️ **The Yes/No trap (crucial for AZ speakers).** English answers the **fact**:
> *"Aren't you hungry?"* → *"**Yes**"* = I **am** hungry · *"**No**"* = I'm **not** hungry.
> Azerbaijani "hə/yox" agrees or disagrees with the **question**, so it flips — don't carry that habit into English. 🇦🇿 *İngiliscə cavab **fakta** bağlıdır, sualın inkarına yox. "Yes" = acam; "No" = ac deyiləm — sualdakı "aren't" cavabı çevirmir.*

## 🅴5 — TAG QUESTIONS: the full matching table

| Statement | Operator used | Tag |
|---|---|---|
| You **are** ready, | be | **aren't** you? |
| You **aren't** ready, | be | **are** you? |
| She **plays** well, | do/does | **doesn't** she? |
| They **didn't** come, | did | **did** they? |
| He **has** left, | have | **hasn't** he? |
| We **can** go, | modal | **can't** we? |
| It **won't** rain, | modal | **will** it? |
| **I'm** right, | am | **aren't I?** |
| **Let's** dance, | *(let's)* | **shall we?** |
| **Sit** down, | *(imperative)* | **will / won't / can you?** |
| **There's** time, | there | **isn't there?** |
| **Nobody** knows, | negative word | **do they?** |
| **Everything's** fine, | *(everything → it)* | **isn't it?** |

> 🔑 Rules: **opposite polarity**, **same operator**, subject → **pronoun**; special cases *aren't I*, *shall we*, *will you*, *there* repeats, *nobody/someone → they*, *nothing/everything → it*. 🇦🇿 *Qaydalar: əks qütb, eyni operator, mübtəda → əvəzlik; xüsusi hallar: aren't I, shall we, will you, there → there, someone → they.*

## 🅴6 — TAG intonation & same-polarity tags

| Tag type | Intonation | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| genuine question | **rising** ↗ | "I'm really asking" — "You locked it, didn't you?↗" |
| seeking agreement | **falling** ↘ | "You agree, right?" — "Lovely day, isn't it?↘" |
| **same-polarity** (+/+) | rising, often sarcastic | "So you're the manager, **are you?**" (surprise/challenge) |

> 🗣️ A **positive-positive** tag (*"You think you're clever, do you?"*) signals **surprise, suspicion or sarcasm** — not the neutral opposite-polarity tag. 🇦🇿 *Müsbət-müsbət tag təəccüb/istehza bildirir; ton məna dəyişir.*

## 🅴7 — INDIRECT / EMBEDDED questions (statement order)

```
   DIRECT (inverted)              INDIRECT (statement order, no do)
   "Where IS it?"             →   "Do you know where it IS?"
   "What TIME does it start?" →   "Can you tell me what time it STARTS?"
   "DID he call?"             →   "I wonder IF/WHETHER he called."
```

| Opener | + wh-/if + statement clause |
|---|---|
| Could you tell me… | …**where the toilets are**? |
| Do you know… | …**if the bank is open**? |
| I wonder… | …**why she said that**. |
| I'm not sure… | …**how much it costs**. |
| Have you any idea… | …**when they're arriving**? |

> ⚠️ Traps: **no inversion** (~~where is it~~ → *where it is*), **no do** (~~what does it mean~~ → *what it means*), **if/whether** for yes/no, and a **question mark only if the main clause is a question**. 🇦🇿 *Dolayı sual: inversiya yox, do yox, bəli/xeyr üçün if/whether, sual işarəsi yalnız əsas cümlə sualdırsa.*

---

## 🅴8 — REPORTED questions (backshift)

When you **report** a question, it becomes indirect **and** back-shifts (like Ch.25 reported speech):

| Direct | Reported |
|---|---|
| "Where **do** you **live**?" | She asked where I **lived**. |
| "**Are** you **coming**?" | He asked **if** I **was coming**. |
| "**Did** you **finish**?" | She asked **whether** I **had finished**. |
| "What **will** you **do**?" | They asked what I **would do**. |

> 🔑 Reported questions use **statement order**, **no do**, **if/whether** for yes/no, and normal **backshift** of tense. Full rules → Reported Speech (Ch.25). 🇦🇿 *Nəql edilən suallar: düz sıra, do yox, if/whether, adi backshift.*

## 🅴9 — Prepositions & *who / whom / whose*

| Informal (stranded) | Formal (fronted) | Note |
|---|---|---|
| **Who** are you talking **to**? | **To whom** are you talking? | preposition + *whom* |
| **What** is it made **of**? | **Of what** is it made? | rare/very formal |
| **Whose** car is this? | — | possession (no shift) |
| **Who**('s) coming? | — | subject → *who* |

> 🔑 *who* = subject/general; *whom* = object/after preposition (formal); *whose* = possessive. Everyday English **strands** the preposition at the end. 🇦🇿 *who (mübtəda), whom (tamamlıq/sözönündən sonra, rəsmi), whose (yiyəlik); danışıqda sözönü sonda qalır.*

## 🅴10 — The *how + …* family & special question frames

| Frame | Asks | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **how long** | duration | **How long** have you lived here? |
| **how often** | frequency | **How often** do you exercise? |
| **how far** | distance | **How far** is the airport? |
| **how much / many** | quantity | **How much** does it cost? |
| **how come** | why (informal, statement order) | **How come** you're back? |
| **what … like** | description | **What**'s the weather **like**? |
| **what … for** | purpose | **What**'s this button **for**? |

> 🗣️ *what … like?* (description) ≠ *how?* (manner/health): *"What's he like?"* (personality) vs *"How is he?"* (health/mood). 🇦🇿 *"What … like?" = təsvir; "How?" = tərz/vəziyyət.*

## 🅴11 — Echo, rhetorical & conversational questions

| Type | Example | Use |
|---|---|---|
| short/echo | "I won." — "**Did you?**" / "**You did?**" | interest / surprise |
| checking | "**Sorry? / Come again? / Say what?**" | ask to repeat |
| rhetorical | "**Who cares?** / **Why bother?**" | no answer expected |
| dramatic echo | "You spent **how much?!**" | shock (wh- stays in place) |
| opener | "**Guess what?** / **You know what?**" | starting news |
| tag-like | "…**right? / yeah? / eh?**" | modern all-purpose tag |

> 🇦🇿 *Danışıq sualları: "Did you?" (maraq), "Sorry?" (təkrar xahişi), "Who cares?" (ritorik), "…right?" (universal tag).*

## 🅴12 — 🇦🇿 CONTRASTIVE SYNTHESIS

| English | 🇦🇿 | The contrast |
|---|---|---|
| **Do** you **like** tea? | Çay **istəyirsənmi**? | AZ: particle **-mi**, no *do*, no inversion |
| **Where do** you **live**? | Harada yaşayırsan? | AZ: sual sözü, söz sırası dəyişmir |
| **Who broke** it? | Onu kim sındırdı? | subject Q — AZ order identical |
| It's cold, **isn't it?** | Soyuqdur, **elə deyil?** | AZ: one fixed tag for all |
| **Aren't** you coming? — **Yes.** (I am) | Gəlmirsən? — **Hə** (gəlirəm) / Yox | ⚠️ opposite Yes/No logic! |
| Do you know **where it is**? | Onun **harada olduğunu** bilirsən? | AZ order ≈ English **indirect** |

> 🧠 **The Azerbaijani contrast in one line:** Azerbaijani asks by **adding** — the particle **-mı/-mi/-mu/-mü** (or a question word) — and **keeps the word order** of a statement. English asks by **re-ordering** — inverting the operator and inserting *do* — and by matching a **different tag** to every sentence. Two consequences for the AZ learner: (1) **direct questions** feel unnatural (why move words and add *do*?), so drill inversion + do-support hard; (2) **indirect questions** feel *easy*, because their statement order is exactly how Azerbaijani builds every question anyway — *"onun harada olduğunu"* = *"where it is."* And never carry the **hə/yox** logic into English negative answers.

# 📖 MINI STORY #2 — "Lost in a New City"

### *Polite indirect questions, a few tags and one puzzled negative — a traveller finds her way.*
### 🇦🇿 *Nəzakətli dolayı suallar, bir neçə tag və bir təəccüblü inkar — səyahətçi yolunu tapır.*

> Read it twice. **First** for the story; **second** circling every question — and note which are **direct** and which are **indirect**.
> 🇦🇿 *İki dəfə oxu: əvvəl hekayə üçün, sonra hər sualı dövrəyə alaraq — hansı birbaşa, hansı dolayıdır?*

---

Maya stepped out of the station into an unfamiliar city. She stopped a friendly-looking man.

"Excuse me — **could you tell me where the old town is**?" *(indirect: 'where the old town is,' not 'where is…'.)*

"Of course. **Do you know which bus goes there**?" he asked. *(indirect.)*

"No, I don't. **Which one should I take**?" *(direct wh-.)*

"The number 7. **It stops right over there, doesn't it**—" he glanced round, "—yes, by the fountain. **You've got a ticket, haven't you?**" *(two tags — checking.)*

"Not yet. **Where do they sell them**?" *(direct object question.)*

"At that kiosk. **Don't** you **have** a travel app?" he asked, surprised. *(negative question — mild surprise.)*

"**No** — my phone died this morning." *(answering the fact: 'No, I don't have one working.')*

"Ah. Well, **I wonder if the kiosk takes cards**," he said. *(indirect — no question mark, it's a statement.)* "**Who's** working today, I wonder…"

At the kiosk, Maya asked, "**Could you tell me how much a day pass costs**?" *(indirect.)*

"Five manats. **Anything else?**" *(short question.)*

"Just one more thing — **do you know when the last bus leaves**?" *(indirect.)*

"Around eleven. You're a tourist, **aren't you?** Enjoy the old town — **who wouldn't**, on a day like this?" *(tag + rhetorical question.)*

Maya laughed, thanked him, and — for the first time all day — felt sure of **exactly where she was going.**

```
1. List the indirect questions. Turn each back into a direct question (restore the inversion).
2. "Who's working today?" — subject or object question? How do you know? Why no 'do'?
3. The man asks "Don't you have a travel app?" and Maya says "No." What does her "No" mean exactly?
4. Add a tag: "You're not from here, ______?" and "Let's find the number 7, ______?"

> 💬 **Answers**
> 1. *"Could you tell me where the old town is?"* → **"Where is the old town?"** · *"Do you know which bus goes there?"* → **"Which bus goes there?"** · *"I wonder if the kiosk takes cards."* → **"Does the kiosk take cards?"** · *"Do you know when the last bus leaves?"* → **"When does the last bus leave?"**
> 2. **Subject question** — *who* is the person doing the working (the subject), so English uses normal order and **no *do***: *"Who's working?"* (= *who is working*).
> 3. Her "No" answers the **fact**: *"No, I don't have a (working) travel app."* — English answers the situation, not the negative in the question.
> 4. *"You're not from here, **are you?**"* (negative statement → positive tag) · *"Let's find the number 7, **shall we?**"*
> 5. Could you tell me where the old town is? / Where do they sell the tickets? / You're a tourist, aren't you?

## 🏁 End of Chapter 31

You can now **ask anything.** English builds questions by **inverting the operator** — a form of *be* or an auxiliary — and by borrowing **do/does/did** when the present or past simple has none (with the main verb back to **base form**). You separate **subject questions** (*"Who called?"* — no *do*) from **object questions** (*"Who did you call?"* — with *do*), soften and provoke with **negative questions** (answering the **fact**, not the polarity), glue conversation with **tag questions** (opposite polarity, matching operator, meaningful **intonation**), and — crucially — switch to **statement word order** inside **indirect questions** (*"…where the station **is**"*), which is exactly where Azerbaijani word order feels most at home. ❓

```
 ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
 │ ❓ yes/no:    invert the operator      "Are you…? Do you…? Have you…?"      │
 │ 🔎 wh-:       Wh- + operator + subject "Where do you live?"                 │
 │ 👤 subject Q: NO do, normal order      "Who broke it?" "What happened?"     │
 │ 🚫 negative:  attitude, answer the FACT "Isn't it lovely?" → Yes(=it is)    │
 │ 🏷️ tag:       opposite polarity, match  "You're in, aren't you?"            │
 │ 📦 indirect:  statement order, no do    "…where the station IS."            │
 │ ⚠️ ~~Where you are going~~ · ~~know where is it~~ · ~~Who did break it~~     │
 └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```
